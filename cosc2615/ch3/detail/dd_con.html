  • In Diamond v. Diehr, whose outcome was the result of a 5-4 decision, a patent was finally awarded for a computer program.
    • In this instance, the program assisted in a process of converting rubber into tires.
  • Critics argued that Diehr had only a new computer program, since all of the parts of the machine used in the conversion process consisted of traditional technology except for the computer program.
  • Although the Court ruled in favor of Diehr, the justices, in their decision, continued to affirm the view that computer algorithms themselves are not patentable.
  • The Court pointed out that the patent awarded to Diehr was not for the computer program but for the rubber tire transformation process as a whole.
